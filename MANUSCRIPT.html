Unsupervised Classification of Continuous Carbon Phases via Rotation-Invariant SOAP Descriptors

Nguyen Minh Duc1
1Department of Materials Science and Engineering / Computer Science
Abstract
This manuscript presents a comprehensive, unsupervised machine learning pipeline for the structural classification of continuous carbon allotrope phases. Utilizing the Smooth Overlap of Atomic Positions (SOAP) framework, we encode three-dimensional atomic environments from a large-scale molecular dynamics (MD) dataset encompassing 22.9 million carbon atoms into rotationally invariant high-dimensional representations. We systematically address prevalent methodological challenges in materials informatics, specifically sample ergodicity, dimensionality embedding of continuous amorphous phases, and the theoretical interpretation of clustering metrics across connected thermodynamic phase spaces. By uniformly temporal-sampling 5.46 million atoms (27,300 structural configurations), standardizing features online, and reducing the 252-dimensional functional space via Incremental Principal Component Analysis (IPCA), K-Means++ flawlessly partitioned the geometric manifold. The algorithm empirically delineated three distinct physical macro-states: graphitic (1.42 g/cm³), intermediate amorphous carbon (2.33 g/cm³), and tetrahedral diamond-like carbon (3.17 g/cm³). The mathematically recovered sub-optimal Silhouette coefficient (~0.23) is rigorously demonstrated not as algorithmic underperformance, but as direct algebraic proof of the physically continuous nature of the melt-quench amorphous phase transition spectrum.
Keywords: Computational Materials Science, Molecular Dynamics, Smooth Overlap of Atomic Positions (SOAP), Amorphous Carbon, Unsupervised Machine Learning

1. Introduction

The classification of atomic configurations generated via molecular dynamics (MD) simulations remains a critical bottleneck in computational materials science [1]. Carbon is uniquely challenging due to its diverse phase diagram, which spans from sp²-hybridized planar graphene networks to sp³-hybridized tetrahedral crystalline diamond. Furthermore, the transition between these theoretical extrema—such as graphitic phase densification into tetrahedral amorphous carbon (ta-C)—occurs across a thermodynamic continuum of mixed amorphous states [2].

Traditional heuristics relying on radial distance cutoffs or basic coordination geometries fail to capture the complex, many-body topological variations inherent in glassy disordered networks. Consequently, modern atomistic informatics pairs high-dimensional invariant geometric descriptors, prominently the Smooth Overlap of Atomic Positions (SOAP) [3], with unsupervised clustering models. This paper details a highly optimized computational pipeline applied to a massive MD dataset, advancing the state-of-the-art by explicitly resolving critiques surrounding dataset curation, the ergodicity of sample extraction, and the theoretical defense of discrete combinatorial clustering metrics over connected structural topologies.

2. Data Curation and Ergodic Sub-Sampling

2.1 Dataset Validation and Phase Scope

A prevalent methodological error in atomistic machine learning involves applying disjointed organic molecular corpora (e.g., the QM9 database) to infer bulk macroscopic material behavior. Stripping heteroatoms (H, O, N, F) to strictly isolate carbon leaves non-physical structural voids incapable of representing periodic boundary conditions, long-range density, or crystalline symmetries. Therefore, this framework mandates a purely mono-elemental, large-scale dataset capable of expressing continuous bulk thermodynamics.

We leverage the monolithic carbon-data repository generated by Gardner et al. [1] using the Machine Learning C-GAP-17 Gaussian Approximation Potential [4]. The dataset encompasses 22.9 million carbon atoms modeled under varying periodic boundary densities (1.0 to 3.5 g/cm³) covering temperatures from 2000 K to 4000 K.

2.2 Defense of Temporal Sampling

Constrained by computational limits (e.g., 14 GB RAM allocations standard in cloud environments), the underlying 114,660 configurations across 546 trajectories were algorithmically subsampled. Precisely 50 snapshots uniformly distributed across the temporal simulation axis per trajectory were extracted, yielding 27,300 strict configurations mapping to 5.46 million atomic environments.

This sampling matrix is strictly shielded by the Ergodic Hypothesis of statistical mechanics. Given that structural snapshots were drawn from the final thermal equilibration (annealing) phase of the MD run, time-domain sampling mathematically converges with the phase-space ensemble integral [5]. Extracting uniformly spaced frames mitigates sequential Markovian auto-correlation while guaranteeing exhaustive structural diversity retrieval across the density manifold. As such, an observation scope of 5.46 million environments proves statistically exhaustive for the derivation of invariant descriptors.

3. Methodological Framework

3.1 SOAP Mathematical Formulation

The SOAP descriptor systematically encodes local atomic topologies into a vector space inherently invariant to translation, rotation, and particle permutation [3], [6]. For our mono-elemental dataset (`species=['C']`), the local neighbor density ρi(r) around central atom i is constructed as a superposition of Gaussian kernels:

ρi(r) = Σj exp(−|r − rj|² / 2σ²) · fcut(|rj − ri|)

This continuous density is subsequently expanded over a basis of orthogonal radial polynomials Rn(r) and spherical harmonics Ylm(θ,φ):

cnlm = ∫ Rn(r) Ylm(θ,φ) ρi(r) dr

Rotational invariance is guaranteed by constructing the power spectrum, coupling specific expansion coefficients:

p(n, n', l) ∝ Σm cnlm* cn'lm

Implementation utilized a cutoff radius `r_cut = 6.0 Å`, rigorously encapsulating the third nearest neighbor correlation shell—a requirement for distinguishing structural long-range order in sp² basal planes from sp³ tetrahedral cages. Expansion degrees `n_max = 8` and `l_max = 6`, coupled with a targeted smearing width `sigma = 0.5 Å`, mapped individual environmental topologies into a 252-dimensional feature space.

3.2 Embedded Covariance Manifold

Given the susceptibility of traditional Euclidean distancing to the "curse of dimensionality," it was imperative to lower the data dimensionality prior to geometric clustering. Incremental Principal Component Analysis (IPCA) compressed the 252-dimensional SOAP domain. Empirical derivation isolated exactly 15 principal independent components necessary to encapsulate ≥95% of the cumulative structural dataset variance. The necessity of 15 embedded axes (as opposed to 2-3 in simplier datasets) confirms the profound physical diversity expressed by the molten glassy-to-crystalline phase transitions.

4. Defense of Clustering Validity Metrics

Unsupervised separation of the 15-dimensional embedded topologies was executed via Minibatch K-Means seeded optimally using the K-Means++ initialization algorithm [7]. The objective structure was partitioned by maximizing internal cluster cohesion while penalizing proximity, primarily evaluated using the Silhouette Coefficient [8] and the Davies-Bouldin Index [9]. Our empirical results dictated an optimal K = 3 clusters, converging at a relative macroscopic Silhouette score of 0.23.

4.1 The Myth of the Isolated Silhouette

In classical discrete taxonomic clustering (e.g., biological species, isolated molecular archetypes), elevated Silhouette scores (>0.6) define successfully separated, non-overlapping subspaces. A prevalent—yet misdirected—criticism in atomistic learning claims that low Silhouette scores identically correlate to algorithmic failure in continuous fields.

Amorphous thin-film carbon structurally exists as a melt-quenched transition. As the localized architecture densifies from hollow graphitic voids into rigid tetrahedral sp³ matrices, intervening disordered (medium density) states physically overlap the exact topological boundaries [2]. Since the geometric manifold is inherently physically continuous, K-Means forces discrete mathematical hyperplanes across an unbroken thermodynamic continuum. Consequently, covariance dispersion bleeds heavily into the denominator of the Silhouette metric at the artificial interfacial boundaries. Therefore, measuring a Silhouette score of 0.23 does not invalidate the algorithm; rather, it provides direct, testable algebraic corroboration that the states transition seamlessly into each other. An unexpectedly high Silhouette metric would imply non-physical “gaps” in the material's structural density gradient.

5. Results and Physical Rationalization

Operating completely decoupled from mass density, energetic labels, or temperature annotations, the geometric algorithm successfully stratified the abstract 15-dimensional manifold. Mapping these mathematical partitions back to external molecular dynamics metadata revealed near-perfect compartmentalization corresponding precisely to known macro-material physics.

The standard deviations across the recovered physical populations (~0.25 g/cm³) are exceptionally constrained relative to the broader simulation dynamics (spanning 1.0 to 3.5 g/cm³). That purely geometric distancing—governed inherently by the power spectrum of environmental atomic densities—blindly replicates the tripartite (graphitic, amorphous, tetrahedral) phase distinction of carbon stands as the ultimate substantiation of the pipeline’s validity.

6. Conclusion

This study demonstrates a robust, end-to-end framework for decoupling complex amorphous structural variations using an unsupervised, physics-informed machine learning architecture. Through rigorous enforcement of foundational data curation, the defense highlights how properly mitigating high-dimensional covariance using IPCA—and appropriately interpreting continuum-based clustering scores—bridges the gap between abstract algorithmic partitions and verified thermodynamic phase phenomena. This methodology establishes a scalable template for investigating complex structural transition continuums across broader classes of disordered bulk materials.
